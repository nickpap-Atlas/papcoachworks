The Machine

Not a replica,
nor a homage,
rather, an evolution.

The 500 occupies a space no other car dares to enter — the intersection of 1970s design philosophy and contemporary supercar engineering. It is what the Countach LP400 might have become, had Bertone been given fifty more years.

Built on the proven architecture of the Lamborghini Gallardo, the structural foundation is authentic: aluminium chassis, mid-engine geometry, drivetrain integrity — untouched. Over this, an entirely new body is sculpted by hand, by eye, by obsession.

Each commission is unique. Each car is a direct collaboration between Nick and the individual who will live with it.

The Man Behind the Machine

Four decades.
One unbroken thread.

Nick Papanicolaou did not decide to become a coachbuilder. He was always going to be one — from the teenager sketching cars in the back of class, to the man who has now spent forty years quietly mastering every discipline required to build them.

His path ran through the family engineering workshop, the Royal Australian Air Force, the Lamborghini Club of Australia, the development of custom parts for his own Urraco P250 and other Countach owners, and through the Spartan Motor Company. Every step was preparation. Nothing was wasted.

The Spartan Era

The world already
knows his work.

Before Papanicolaou Coachworks, there was Spartan Motor Company — co-founded with his twin brother Peter and Nick's son (Nick Jnr). What began as a design exercise powered by a Ducati 1198S engine was refined through years of development and iteration — evolving through successive prototypes, full CAD engineering, and ultimately into the supercharged, carbon-bodied machine validated on track against the world's finest.

The Spartan broke an eleven-year lap record at Rockingham Motor Speedway. It placed second at the EVO Track Car of the Year Awards 2024. It was featured multiple times by Top Gear magazine and driven by The Stig. Documented by Petrol Ped and Cinch. These are not claims — they are a record of proof.

HISTORY

A lifelong obsession is born.

Sydney, mid-1980s

Nick grew up haunting car dealerships on weekends, dragging his brother and cousin from showroom to showroom in search of anything rare, fast, or foreign. It was instinct more than ambition — an unspoken pull toward machines that looked like they didn't belong on ordinary roads.

The moment that changed everything came from a magazine cover. A Lamborghini Countach stared back from the front of CAR Magazine — angular, aggressive, and completely uncompromising. For Nick, it wasn't just a supercar. It was a statement of design philosophy: that form could be radical, presence could be confrontational, and emotion could be engineered.

That image triggered years of focused sketching and self-directed study. By the mid-1980s, the obsession had produced something tangible: a series of limited-edition automotive art prints, exhibited and sold publicly at the Sydney Motor Show. It was the first time Nick's passion found an audience — and the first step in what would become a lifelong pursuit.

HISTORY

Craft in metal.

From sketches to steel

After completing a degree in graphic design, Nick returned to the family engineering business his father (pictured right) had built, and found an entirely different kind of education waiting for him. Under his close mentorship, he developed hands-on fluency in machining, fabrication, and mechanical design.

Around the same time, his first significant build project took shape: a motorcycle-powered dune buggy, purpose-built to explore power-to-weight dynamics and hands-on fabrication. Lightweight, raw, and running on aviation fuel, it was an unfiltered introduction to performance engineering.

Countless additional weekends were spent inside a cousin's Lamborghini workshop, observing everything from routine servicing to complete engine rebuilds. These years provided direct exposure to professional-level tolerances, materials, and high-performance engineering practice.

The workshop became the proving ground where creative vision was translated into physical reality. It was here that Nick formed the principle that still defines Papanicolaou Coachworks today: great design must be supported by engineering discipline, precision, and repeatable craftsmanship.

HISTORY

Shaping skills.

Discipline, community, and keeping the passion alive

Nick's technical foundation was further refined during service with the Royal Australian Air Force as a Communications Technician. The military environment reinforced structured problem-solving, systems thinking, and disciplined execution — qualities that continue to underpin every commissioned build at Papaniclaou Coachworks.

In parallel, his involvement with the Lamborghini Club of Australia went well beyond membership. Nick personally designed and produced the club's periodical magazine, The Bull's Roar — managing both its visual identity and print production across multiple editions.

This role deepened his relationships within the Australian Lamborghini community and gave him first-hand insight into collector culture, marque heritage, and the exacting standards of serious enthusiasts. It also sharpened a skill that would prove essential: the ability to communicate design with clarity and conviction.

HISTORY

First Lamborghini.

Craft meets heritage

Years of parallel development — engineering, design, and community — culminated in Nick's first Lamborghini acquisition: a Urraco P250S. True to form, simple ownership was never the goal. The car became a technical project.

A custom electronic ignition system was engineered and fabricated from scratch. Bespoke wheels inspired by the Bravo concept were created to complement the car's stance and visual character. Both solutions were subsequently adopted by other Urraco and Countach owners — an early signal that Nick's approach to blending modern engineering with respect for classic design language resonated far beyond his own garage.

This period marked a clear transition: from private builder and passionate enthusiast to recognised specialist. The reputation that Papanicolaou Coachworks carries today was quietly being earned, one precisely engineered component at a time.

HISTORY

Spartan Motor Company

From specialist craftsmanship to global recognition

The Spartan era marked a decisive step from private capability into recognised, world-class execution. What had been built over decades through design discipline, fabrication, engineering thinking and mechanical fluency was now being expressed through a machine that drew international attention.

The car began as a design exercise — a shared obsession between Nick and his twin brother Peter to build something that delivered motorcycle levels of performance on four wheels. The first prototype was powered by the V-twin engine from a Ducati 1198S, producing around 147bhp in a car weighing approximately 450kg. Testing at a private facility revealed spectacular performance and chassis balance, but after several months it was decided to move to a more reliable and powerful powertrain. A Honda Jazz engine was sourced and supercharged, developing 210bhp — and the car evolved from a shed prototype into a fully engineered machine.

The production Spartan went further still — a supercharged 2.4-litre Honda-derived engine producing around 400bhp, a tubular steel space frame and carbon fibre body tipping the scales at just 700kg, with aero developed with the assistance of a designer with Formula 1 experience across Williams, Toyota, and Caterham. Production has been intentionally limited to 300 examples, a nod to the 300 Spartans of antiquity.
